## Test 4: M-Component Semantic Correlation

Does the algebraic L2=yin member correspond to the reader's semantically "more receptive" member?

| Pair | L2=yin Member | Reader's "Receptive" | Match? |
|:---:|---|---|:---:|

| 25 (Ge/Ding) | **Ge** | **Ding** | **✗** |

**Result: 5/6 match (83%).** The one mismatch (Ge/Ding) is significant.

### The Ge/Ding Mismatch

Ge (#49, Revolution) has L2=yin. The reader identified Ge as the *active* member (revolutionary destruction) and Ding as the *receptive* member (consolidating vessel). So L2=yin does not always correspond to the semantically receptive hexagram.

**What this reveals:** The M-component (L2=yin) captures something about the *internal structure* of the hexagram — its inner ruler is receptive — not about whether the hexagram as a whole is the more receptive member of its pair. A hexagram can have a "receptive within" structure (L2=yin) while being the more dynamically active member of its pair. Revolution has yin at its inner core even as it destroys outwardly.

This is a genuine finding: **the M-component and the semantic activity/receptivity axis are correlated but not identical.** The correlation (5/6 = 83%) is strong enough to matter, but the exception proves they are distinct dimensions.

## Test 5: The "Active First" Correction

### Round 1's finding: "Active first"

All 3 Round 1 pairs (M-rule exceptions) placed the more active, outward-directed member first.

### Round 2's correction: "Condition first"

Round 2 reveals this was an artifact of the exception pairs. The broader pattern across all 11 pairs:

| Pattern Type | First Member Character | Pairs |
|---|---|---|
| Active → Receptive | Active initiator first | Shi→Bi, Tai→Pi, Lin→Guan, Ge→Ding, Zhen→Gen |
| Problem → Solution | Suffering/blocked first | Jian→Xie, Bo→Fu, Kun→Jing |
| Discipline → Growth | Sacrificial discipline first | Sun→Yi |
| Withdrawal → Advance | Strategic retreat first | Dun→Da Zhuang |
| Danger → Clarity | Peril first | Kan→Li |

**The unifying principle is developmental priority:** whatever creates the conditions for the other member comes first — regardless of whether that condition is active (Revolution, Thunder) or receptive/suffering (Oppression, Obstruction, Retreat).

This is a more precise characterization than "active first" and explains why both the M-rule hits and exceptions work. At M-rule conforming pairs, the "condition" (which comes first) tends to have L2=yin (receptive within). At M-rule exception pairs, the "condition" has L2=yang. The semantic ordering principle operates independently of the M-component.

## Key Findings

### 1. Meaning alignment is statistically significant
11/11 (p < 0.001). The null is rejected. KW's ordering correlates with meaning-level properties that the reader can independently identify.

### 2. All 4 M-rule exceptions are meaning-justified
The algebraic M-rule breaks at pairs where meaning demands the opposite ordering. These are not noise — they are semantic overrides. KW resolves the conflict between the M-component and semantic ordering in favor of meaning.

### 3. The semantic principle is "condition first," not "active first"
The initiating condition precedes its consequence, whether the condition is active (Revolution, Thunder, Mobilization) or passive (Obstruction, Decrease, Retreat, Splitting Apart). This is a developmental ordering principle, not an activity/receptivity principle.

### 4. M-component ≈ but ≠ semantic receptivity
L2=yin correlates with the reader's "receptive" identification 83% of the time (5/6), but the Ge/Ding mismatch shows they are distinct properties. The M-component captures an internal structural quality ("receptive within") that is related to but not identical with the hexagram's overall character in its pair.

### 5. The Xugua is confirming, not driving
8/8 "Clear" pairs have orderings that follow from meanings alone, without needing the Xugua's post-hoc rationalization.

## What Remains

1. **21 pairs unassessed.** The remaining pairs need coverage to test whether the alignment rate holds or degrades. Particularly interesting:
   - The other M-conforming pairs in the lower canon
   - The remaining complement pairs (Qian/Kun, Yi/Da Guo, Zhong Fu/Xiao Guo)
   - Pairs where the reader might find "no prediction" — these silences would be informative

2. **Can meaning predict WHICH M-rule exception pairs are exceptions?** We know 4/16 M-decisive pairs are exceptions. Does the semantic ordering principle identify the same 4? This is a sharper test: not "does meaning justify the exception?" (confirmed) but "does meaning *predict* where exceptions will occur?"

3. **Falsification targets:**
   - If remaining pairs show alignment < 50%, the current result is specific to an easy subset
   - If "Suggestive" and "Silent" predictions accumulate, the prediction power may be an artifact of selecting pairs with clear developmental logic
   - The hardest test: pairs where meaning genuinely gives no ordering prediction

---

## Falsifiability Status

The null hypothesis (meaning independent of ordering) **is rejected** at p < 0.001 for the current 11-pair sample. The finding is: KW's orientation is significantly aligned with meaning-level properties.

However, several concerns remain:
- **Selection bias**: Were these 11 pairs chosen because they have clear developmental logic? If the remaining 21 pairs are harder to read, the alignment rate may drop.
- **Confirmability asymmetry**: It may be easier to construct a rationale for a known ordering than to genuinely predict it blind. The reader's "blindness" to algebra doesn't guarantee blindness to conventional ordering knowledge.
- **The Xugua confound**: Even restricting to "Clear" pairs, the reader's knowledge of traditional commentary may embed implicit ordering knowledge.

The investigation needs the remaining pairs — especially those where meaning might be genuinely silent — to test whether the alignment holds or was an easy-subset artifact.
