# Round 2: Reader Assessment — Broadening Across the Landscape

Reading 8 hexagram pairs spanning multiple categories: the 4th M-rule exception, M-rule conforming pairs, non-M-decisive pairs, and one complement pair. Assessment based purely on meanings, names, judgments (卦辭), images (象辭), Xugua (序卦傳), Zagua (雜卦), and the broader commentarial tradition.

---

## Pair 1: Sun 損 (#41, Decrease) / Yi 益 (#42, Increase)
**Category: 4th M-rule exception (lower canon)**
**KW order: Sun first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Sun (損)** — Decrease / Diminution / Loss. The judgment reads: "Decrease combined with sincerity brings about supreme good fortune without blame. One may be persevering in this. It furthers one to undertake something. How is this to be carried out? One may use two small bowls for the sacrifice." Sun describes a situation of voluntary reduction — giving up, paring down, simplifying. The character 損 means to diminish, reduce, or sacrifice. The Daxiang says: "At the foot of the mountain, the lake: the image of Decrease. Thus the superior man controls his anger and restrains his instincts." This is a discipline of letting go — restraining excess in the lower (instincts, desires) to benefit the higher.

**Yi (益)** — Increase / Augmentation / Gain. The judgment reads: "Increase. It furthers one to undertake something. It furthers one to cross the great water." Yi describes a situation of growth, enrichment, enhancement. The character 益 means to add to, to benefit, to profit. The Daxiang says: "Wind and thunder: the image of Increase. Thus the superior man, if he sees good, imitates it; if he has faults, he rids himself of them." This is about absorbing benefit from outside, growing by recognizing and correcting.

### Directionality: Sun → Yi (clear, but with a twist)

This is the most philosophically interesting pair in the assignment. The naive expectation is "Increase first, Decrease second" — prosperity before decline, paralleling Tai/Pi. But the Yijing's logic here is precisely inverted: **decrease precedes increase**. The principle is: you must first diminish the excessive, the bloated, the unworthy, in order to create space for genuine growth. This is the economic/spiritual law that sacrifice (giving up the lower) produces genuine gain (growth of the higher).

The Xici (Great Treatise) explicitly discusses this pair together: "Sun and Yi are the beginning of fullness and decline" (損益盛衰之始也). More precisely, Sun is the *beginning* — the initiating movement that makes fullness possible. You must empty before you can fill.

This is not the same as Tai→Pi (flourishing then decline). It is the opposite logic: pruning → flowering. The tradition is unambiguous: Sun is the *condition* for Yi. You decrease first in order to increase.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "解者緩也。緩必有所失，故受之以損。" — "Deliverance (Xie, #40) means relaxation. When things relax, something is inevitably lost. Therefore Decrease follows." Then: "損而不已必益，故受之以益。" — "Decrease that does not cease must eventually turn to Increase. Therefore Increase follows." 

The Xugua's logic is explicit: unceasing decrease transforms into increase. Sun is the causal antecedent; Yi is the natural consequence. The movement from letting go to gaining is presented as inevitable — decrease carried to its proper completion *becomes* increase.

### Activity / Receptivity

This is genuinely nuanced and may not fit a simple active/receptive frame.

**Sun is more active in a disciplinary sense.** It requires deliberate effort — you must *choose* to reduce, restrain, sacrifice. Controlling anger, restraining instincts, offering what you have even when it's little. It is effortful inward discipline. The Daxiang's instruction — control anger, restrain desires — is an active, volitional practice.

**Yi is more receptive in a growth sense.** Increase comes by receiving — imitating the good one sees, absorbing lessons, crossing the great water (venturing out to receive what the world offers). There is an openness to Yi, a quality of *taking in* what has been made possible by Sun's clearing.

However, one could argue the reverse: Sun is passive yielding (letting go) and Yi is active growth (undertaking, crossing the great water). This pair does not map cleanly onto active/receptive in the way Shi/Bi or Tai/Pi do. The more precise characterization: Sun is *preparatory discipline* and Yi is *consequent flourishing*. The discipline is prior.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Zagua says: "損益盛衰之始也" — "Decrease and Increase are the beginning of fullness and decline." This famous formulation treats them as the two movements that initiate cycles. Critically, the Zagua lists Sun first — the downward/emptying movement initiates, the upward/filling movement follows.

Cheng Yi treats Sun as the active practice of self-cultivation and Yi as its fruit. Wang Bi reads the pair as illustrating the principle that you cannot increase without first decreasing — fullness comes from emptiness, not from adding more to what is already full. Zhu Xi treats them as the natural pair marking the turning point between waning and waxing.

The Xici passage "損先難而後易" ("Decrease is first difficult, then easy") explicitly treats Sun as the prior, harder movement that enables the subsequent easier one.

### Confidence: **Clear**

Every layer of the tradition — Xugua, Zagua, Xici, and major commentators — consistently treats Sun as prior to Yi. The logic is not "active first" but "discipline first, growth follows." Decrease → Increase is presented as both temporally prior and causally necessary.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Sun first**

The tradition is unambiguous: you must decrease before you can increase. Sacrifice enables growth. Emptying enables filling. Sun → Yi is presented as a fundamental law, not an arbitrary sequence.

## Pair 2: Dun 遯 (#33, Retreat) / Da Zhuang 大壯 (#34, Great Power)
**Category: M-rule conforming (lower canon)**
**KW order: Dun first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Dun (遯)** — Retreat / Withdrawal / Hiding. The judgment reads: "Retreat. Success. In what is small, perseverance furthers." Dun describes the strategic withdrawal from an unfavorable situation — knowing when to pull back, hide, conserve one's strength. The character 遯 means to flee, escape, withdraw. The Daxiang says: "Under heaven there is the mountain: the image of Retreat. Thus the superior man keeps the inferior man at a distance, not angrily but with reserve." This is the wisdom of timely disengagement.

**Da Zhuang (大壯)** — Great Power / The Power of the Great / Great Vigor. The judgment reads: "The Power of the Great. Perseverance furthers." Da Zhuang describes the situation of maximal strength, vigorous forward movement, the force of greatness advancing. The Daxiang says: "Thunder in heaven above: the image of The Power of the Great. Thus the superior man does not tread upon paths that do not accord with established order." This is the moment of peak power — but the instruction warns against misusing it.

### Directionality: Dun → Da Zhuang (suggestive, with tension)

This is a less obvious pairing than the Round 1 pairs. The naive reading might be "power first, retreat later" — you advance, then you withdraw when conditions change. But the KW ordering places retreat first.

The traditional reading follows the seasonal/cyclical model: Dun corresponds to the sixth lunar month (when yin is growing, yang retreating) and Da Zhuang to the second month (when yang is surging). In the annual cycle, retreat precedes the surge of power — winter precedes spring, withdrawal precedes advance. You gather your forces in retreat, then deploy them in power.

However, this seasonal logic is somewhat circular — you could just as well start at any point in the cycle. The more substantive reading: **retreat is the wise preparation for the proper exercise of power.** One who has not learned when to withdraw will misuse great power. The Daxiang's warning in Da Zhuang (not treading improper paths) implies that the wisdom of restraint learned in Dun is the precondition for properly wielding Da Zhuang's vigor.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "物不可以久居其所，故受之以遯。遯者退也。" — "Things cannot forever dwell in their place; therefore Retreat follows." (This follows Heng/Duration.) Then: "物不可以終遯，故受之以大壯。" — "Things cannot retreat forever; therefore Great Power follows."

The Xugua explicitly frames Da Zhuang as the consequence of Dun — retreat cannot go on indefinitely; power surges back. Dun is the condition; Da Zhuang is what inevitably follows when the retreat has run its course.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Dun is receptive/inward.** It is withdrawal, conservation, yielding to circumstances. The mode is strategic passivity — not engaging, maintaining distance, preserving reserves.

**Da Zhuang is active/outward.** It is the surge of power, advancing vigor, force moving forward. Thunder above heaven — maximal active energy.

This pair maps cleanly onto the receptive→active pattern. KW places the receptive member first.

But notice: this is the *opposite* of the Round 1 pattern, where the *active* member came first. Here the *receptive/withdrawing* member comes first, followed by the active/powerful one. This is a genuine difference from the Round 1 pairs.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The seasonal correspondence (Dun = 6th month, Da Zhuang = 2nd month) is well-established in the Han-dynasty commentarial tradition, though the mapping isn't straightforward — the months are not consecutive. The more compelling logic is the Xugua's: retreat turns into advance, withdrawal generates the conditions for the surge.

The Zagua says: "大壯則止，遯則退也" — "Great Power means to stop [i.e., halt or hold firm]; Retreat means to withdraw." This is less helpful for ordering — it characterizes the two situations but doesn't imply a sequence.

### Confidence: **Suggestive**

The Xugua clearly frames Dun→Da Zhuang as the intended sequence, and the logic (retreat exhausts itself, power surges back) is coherent. But the meaning-level case is weaker than the Round 1 pairs. The cycle could logically start at either point. The predominant traditional reading supports Dun first, but the reasoning is more about cyclical inevitability than about a natural developmental priority.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Dun first** (with less conviction than Round 1 pairs)

The Xugua's logic supports Dun → Da Zhuang. The "receptive/withdrawing before active/advancing" pattern here is clear but is the *reverse* of the "active first" pattern seen in the Round 1 exception pairs.

## Pair 3: Jian 蹇 (#39, Obstruction) / Xie 解 (#40, Deliverance)
**Category: M-rule conforming (lower canon)**
**KW order: Jian first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Jian (蹇)** — Obstruction / Difficulty / Limping / Impediment. The judgment reads: "Obstruction. The southwest furthers. The northeast does not further. It furthers one to see the great man. Perseverance brings good fortune." Jian describes the experience of being stuck, impeded, unable to advance — like trying to walk on a lame leg. The Daxiang says: "Water on the mountain: the image of Obstruction. Thus the superior man turns his attention to himself and molds his character." This is the classic "turn inward when the outer way is blocked."

**Xie (解)** — Deliverance / Release / Untangling / Liberation. The judgment reads: "Deliverance. The southwest furthers. If there is no longer anything where one has to go, return brings good fortune. If there is still something where one has to go, hastening brings good fortune." Xie describes the moment when the obstruction breaks, the tension releases, the way clears. The Daxiang says: "Thunder and rain arouse: the image of Deliverance. Thus the superior man pardons mistakes and forgives misdeeds." This is the act of letting go of what has been oppressive.

### Directionality: Jian → Xie (clear)

This is one of the most natural pairings in the entire sequence: first you encounter the obstacle, then you overcome it. Obstruction → Deliverance. Being stuck → breaking free. This is an unmistakable condition → resolution sequence.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "乖必有難，故受之以蹇。蹇者難也。物不可以終難，故受之以解。解者緩也。" — "Opposition (Kui, #38) necessarily leads to difficulty; therefore Obstruction follows. Obstruction means difficulty. Things cannot remain in difficulty forever; therefore Deliverance follows. Deliverance means loosening/release."

The Xugua is maximally explicit: difficulty cannot persist indefinitely, so release follows. Jian is the condition; Xie is the resolution. This is perhaps the clearest causal sequence in the entire Xugua.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Jian is receptive/inward.** You are blocked, so you turn inward, cultivate character, wait. The mode is endurance, patience, self-reflection. You cannot act outwardly because the way is obstructed.

**Xie is active/outward.** Release, loosening, pardoning — these are outward-directed actions. Thunder and rain break the tension. The mode is *doing* — hastening, pardoning, freeing.

Pattern: receptive (enduring the block) → active (breaking free). Same pattern as Pair 2. KW places the receptive/enduring member first.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

This is universally treated as a paradigmatic condition→resolution pair. Cheng Yi: when difficulty is at its extreme, release must follow. Wang Bi: the way through obstruction is to understand the obstruction, then the release comes naturally. The Zagua says: "蹇，難也。解，緩也" — "Jian is difficulty. Xie is ease." This simple contrast naturally implies the sequence: difficulty then ease.

### Confidence: **Clear**

The most natural problem → solution sequence in the Yijing. No commentator reverses this. Obstruction must precede Deliverance.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Jian first**

Obstruction before Deliverance. This is as natural as "question before answer."

## Pair 4: Ge 革 (#49, Revolution) / Ding 鼎 (#50, The Cauldron)
**Category: M-rule conforming (lower canon)**
**KW order: Ge first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Ge (革)** — Revolution / Radical Change / Molting / Skinning. The judgment reads: "Revolution. On your own day you are believed. Supreme success, furthering through perseverance. Remorse disappears." Ge describes the act of fundamental transformation — overthrowing the old, stripping away what has become corrupt, changing the mandate. The character 革 originally means animal hide/leather — the radical transformation of raw material. The Daxiang says: "Fire in the lake: the image of Revolution. Thus the superior man sets the calendar in order and makes the seasons clear." This is an act of fundamental reordering.

**Ding (鼎)** — The Cauldron / The Sacrificial Vessel. The judgment reads: "The Cauldron. Supreme good fortune. Success." Ding describes the sacred vessel used for sacrifice and nourishment — the container in which raw materials are transformed into something that nourishes and sustains. The character 鼎 is a pictograph of the ancient ritual bronze vessel. The Daxiang says: "Fire over wood: the image of The Cauldron. Thus the superior man consolidates his fate by making his position correct." This is about establishing the new order, the vessel that holds and transforms.

### Directionality: Ge → Ding (clear)

This is treated by the tradition as one of the most deliberately structured pairs in the sequence. Ge is the destructive/transformative phase — tearing down the old order. Ding is the constructive/consolidating phase — establishing the new vessel, the new institutional framework. Revolution must precede the establishment of the new order. You cannot fill a cauldron you haven't yet cast.

The political reading is explicit: Ge = overthrowing the Shang dynasty; Ding = establishing the Zhou dynasty's sacrificial and political institutions. The pair encodes the Zhou founding narrative itself.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "井道不可不革，故受之以革。革物者莫若鼎，故受之以鼎。" — "The way of the Well cannot avoid being renewed; therefore Revolution follows. Nothing transforms things like the Cauldron; therefore The Cauldron follows."

The Xugua's logic: revolution (renewal, radical change) creates the need for a vessel to hold and process the transformation. Ge is the act of change; Ding is the instrument that makes the change productive. Revolution without a cauldron is mere destruction; the Cauldron without revolution would have nothing new to transform.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Ge is active and disruptive.** Revolution, overthrow, radical change — these are outward, forceful, yang actions. Fire in the lake — opposed elements creating transformative tension.

**Ding is receptive and consolidating.** The Cauldron receives, contains, transforms. It is the vessel — inherently receptive (it holds what is placed within it) yet transformative. Fire over wood — sustained, patient transformation rather than explosive change.

Pattern: active (revolution) → receptive (consolidation). This matches the Round 1 "active first" pattern.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy on the Yijing notes that "for centuries, the Yijing's commentators heeded the Xugua authors' advice to read Ge and Ding together as two phases of a regime change." Wang Bi and Cheng Yi both read this pair as the dual phases of political transformation: destruction of the old (Ge) followed by construction of the new (Ding). This is perhaps the most politically significant pair in the entire sequence, encoding the Zhou ideology of the Mandate of Heaven.

The Zagua says: "革，去故也。鼎，取新也。" — "Revolution means removing the old. The Cauldron means taking up the new." This is an explicit before→after pair: out with the old, in with the new.

### Confidence: **Clear**

The Xugua, Zagua, and the entire commentarial tradition from Wang Bi through Zhu Xi unanimously treat this as destruction → construction, revolution → institution. No alternative ordering has been proposed.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Ge first**

Revolution before the Cauldron. Tearing down before building up. Removing the old before establishing the new.

## Pair 5: Kun 困 (#47, Oppression) / Jing 井 (#48, The Well)
**Category: Non-M-decisive (lower canon)**
**KW order: Kun first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Kun (困)** — Oppression / Exhaustion / Entrapment / Adversity. The judgment reads: "Oppression. Success. Perseverance. The great man brings about good fortune. No blame. When one has something to say, it is not believed." Kun describes the experience of being squeezed, depleted, confined — your resources are exhausted and your words are not heard. The Daxiang says: "There is no water in the lake: the image of Exhaustion. Thus the superior man stakes his life on following his will." This is an extreme situation demanding inner fortitude.

**Jing (井)** — The Well / The Source. The judgment reads: "The Well. The town may be changed, but the well cannot be changed. It neither decreases nor increases. They come and go and draw from the well. If one gets down almost to the water and the rope does not go all the way, or the jug breaks, it brings misfortune." Jing describes the enduring source — the deep, constant, nourishing resource that remains even when everything else changes. The character 井 is a pictograph of a well. The Daxiang says: "Water over wood: the image of The Well. Thus the superior man encourages the people at their work, and exhorts them to help one another."

### Directionality: Kun → Jing (suggestive)

The progression is from exhaustion/oppression to discovering the enduring source. When you are depleted (Kun), you must find the well (Jing). Oppression drives you to seek the deeper, unchanging resource. The Well is what sustains through and beyond oppression.

There is also a structural logic: Kun describes a situation where resources are gone (no water in the lake — the surface source has dried up). Jing describes the resource that endures when surface sources fail (the well's water comes from below, inexhaustible). Oppression reveals the need for the well; the well is the answer to oppression's depletion.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "升而不已必困，故受之以困。困乎上者必反下，故受之以井。" — "Rising without cease must lead to Exhaustion; therefore Oppression follows. What is oppressed above must turn back downward; therefore The Well follows."

The Xugua's logic: exhaustion (being oppressed above, stuck at the surface) drives one downward toward the deep source. Kun creates the impetus to dig deep; Jing is what you find there. This is an explicit directional claim: oppression → the well.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Kun is more reactive/contracted.** Under oppression, one's scope narrows, one conserves, one is pressed inward. The Daxiang's instruction — staking one's life on one's will — describes the inner fortress under siege.

**Jing is receptive in a deep, sustaining sense.** The well does not act — it is there to be drawn from. It neither increases nor decreases. It is the ultimate receptive image: the unchanging source that gives to whoever comes.

Neither member is straightforwardly "active." Both describe conditions rather than actions. But Kun is the *problem* and Jing is the *resource* — and problems precede their solutions.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

Cheng Yi reads the pair as the complementary relationship between exhaustion and renewal: when you are exhausted, look for the inexhaustible. Wang Bi emphasizes the directionality: what is oppressed above must seek below, and below is the well.

The Zagua says: "井通而困相遇也" — "The Well leads through, while Oppression means encountering." This is somewhat cryptic but suggests that Kun (encountering/meeting adversity) comes before Jing (finding the passage through).

### Confidence: **Suggestive**

The Xugua provides clear directional logic (oppression drives you to the well). The meaning-level case is solid but less overwhelming than Ge/Ding or Jian/Xie because both hexagrams describe *conditions* rather than *actions*, making the developmental sequence less viscerally obvious.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Kun first**

Oppression before the Well. Exhaustion drives you to find the enduring source. The Xugua's directional logic is clear.

## Pair 6: Zhen 震 (#51, Arousing Thunder) / Gen 艮 (#52, Keeping Still)
**Category: Non-M-decisive (lower canon)**
**KW order: Zhen first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Zhen (震)** — The Arousing / Thunder / Shock. The judgment reads: "Shock brings success. Shock comes — Loss! Loss! Laughing words — Surprise! Surprise! Shock terrifies for a hundred miles, and he does not let fall the sacrificial spoon and chalice." Zhen describes the sudden, startling eruption of energy — the thunderclap, the earthquake, the sudden shake that jolts you awake. The Daxiang says: "Thunder repeated: the image of Shock. Thus in fear and trembling the superior man sets his life in order and examines himself." This is awakening through disruption.

**Gen (艮)** — Keeping Still / The Mountain / Stopping. The judgment reads: "Keeping Still. Keeping his back still so that he no longer feels his body. He goes into his courtyard and does not see his people. No blame." Gen describes the practice of stillness, stopping, holding fast — coming to rest at the proper point. The Daxiang says: "Mountains standing close together: the image of Keeping Still. Thus the superior man does not permit his thoughts to go beyond his situation." This is the discipline of knowing when to stop.

### Directionality: Zhen → Gen (clear)

This is one of the most elegant conceptual pairings in the Yijing. Thunder and Mountain, Movement and Stillness, Arousal and Rest — these are the two poles of all dynamic existence. The question is: which comes first?

The tradition's answer is unambiguous: **movement precedes rest.** You must first be shaken awake (Zhen) before you can properly come to rest (Gen). Stillness that has not been preceded by arousal is mere inertia, not genuine composure. The superior man is first *shocked* into examining himself, then *stills* himself to integrate the lesson.

The Zagua confirms: "震，起也。艮，止也。" — "Zhen means to arise. Gen means to stop." Arising precedes stopping. You must first arise before you can properly stop.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "主器者莫若長子，故受之以震。震者動也。物不可以終動，故受之以艮。艮者止也。" — "No one is better suited to be master of the vessels than the eldest son; therefore Shock follows. Shock means movement. Things cannot be in perpetual movement; therefore Keeping Still follows. Keeping Still means stopping."

The Xugua's logic: movement cannot continue forever; it must find its resting point. Zhen is the initiating dynamic; Gen is the natural culmination. Motion → Rest.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Zhen is maximally active.** Shock, thunder, sudden movement, eruption — pure yang activity. Movement itself.

**Gen is maximally receptive/still.** Stopping, holding, the mountain standing firm — pure yin stillness (in the sense of cessation rather than yielding). Rest itself.

Pattern: active → receptive. Same as Round 1 (active first).

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

Cheng Yi and the entire Yixue tradition treat Zhen/Gen as the fundamental movement/stillness pair, paralleling Qian/Kun (creative/receptive) at the trigram level. Zhen (the eldest son) is the first eruption of yang from yin — the *beginning* of the cycle. Gen (the youngest son) is the final consolidation of yang — the *resting point* of the cycle. Beginning before ending, start before stop.

The cosmological correspondence: Zhen = spring, the beginning of growth; Gen = late winter, the end of the cycle and the mountain that holds still before the next spring. Even in this cyclical model, the active/initiating moment is placed first.

### Confidence: **Clear**

Movement before stillness, arising before stopping — this is one of the most fundamental pairings in the Yi, and the ordering is treated as self-evident by the tradition.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Zhen first**

The Arousing before Keeping Still. Movement before rest. Arising before stopping.

## Pair 7: Bo 剝 (#23, Splitting Apart) / Fu 復 (#24, Return)
**Category: Non-M-decisive (upper canon)**
**KW order: Bo first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Bo (剝)** — Splitting Apart / Stripping / Peeling / Disintegration. The judgment reads: "Splitting Apart. It does not further one to go anywhere." Bo describes the process of being stripped away, eroded, disintegrated from below. Things are falling apart. The creative force is being eaten away. The Daxiang says: "The mountain rests on the earth: the image of Splitting Apart. Thus those above can ensure their position only by giving generously to those below." This is the moment of maximal dissolution — everything is being taken away.

**Fu (復)** — Return / The Turning Point / Coming Back. The judgment reads: "Return. Success. Going out and coming in without error. Friends come without blame. The way goes and returns. On the seventh day comes return. It furthers one to have somewhere to go." Fu describes the moment when the light returns — after the darkest point, the first glimmer of renewal. The Daxiang says: "Thunder in the earth: the image of the Turning Point. Thus the kings of antiquity closed the passes at the time of solstice. Merchants and strangers did not go about, and the ruler did not travel through the provinces." This is the winter solstice moment — the deepest point of darkness from which light begins its return.

### Directionality: Bo → Fu (clear, paradigmatic)

This is perhaps the most famous sequential pair in the entire Yijing after Tai/Pi. Bo represents the nadir — the stripping away reaches its extreme. Fu represents the turning point — from the absolute bottom, the first movement of return begins. Dark night of the soul → dawn.

The principle is cosmological: when yin has fully consumed yang (Bo — only one yang line remains at the top, about to be stripped), the cycle reverses and yang begins to return from below (Fu — one yang line appears at the bottom). This is the winter solstice principle: the longest night is followed by the return of light.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "物不可以終盡剝，窮上反下，故受之以復。" — "Things cannot be forever stripped away until nothing remains. What is exhausted above returns below; therefore Return follows."

The Xugua is explicit: complete stripping away is impossible — at the extreme of dissolution, return begins. Bo creates the condition for Fu. Dissolution → renewal.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Bo is passive/dissolving.** Things are being stripped away; you cannot act, you cannot go anywhere. It is the experience of powerless dissolution.

**Fu is gently active.** The return is the first stirring of new energy — thunder stirring within the earth. It is the first movement, the first seed of renewal. Though the mode is gentle and the Daxiang instructs rest and waiting, the essential quality is *initiation* — something new is beginning.

Pattern: this pair doesn't map neatly onto active/receptive. Both members are relatively subdued. Bo is the *condition* (dissolution), Fu is the *response* (renewal). Condition before response.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Bo/Fu pair is one of the most celebrated in the Yijing, extensively discussed in the Song-dynasty Neo-Confucian tradition as illustrating the principle that the Way never dies — at its darkest moment, the light returns. Zhou Dunyi's Taijitu Shuo (Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate) draws on this pair's logic. Cheng Yi treats Fu as the most important hexagram for understanding the nature of heaven-and-earth, because it shows the moment when the moral force reasserts itself.

The Zagua says: "剝，爛也。復，反也。" — "Bo means decay/rotting. Fu means returning/reverting." Decay before return — natural sequence.

### Confidence: **Clear**

Stripping apart before return. The dark before the dawn. This is one of the most unambiguous sequences in the Yijing.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Bo first**

Splitting Apart before Return. Dissolution before renewal. The tradition is unanimous.

## Pair 8: Kan 坎 (#29, Abysmal Water) / Li 離 (#30, Clinging Fire)
**Category: Complement pair (different pairing logic)**
**KW order: Kan first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Kan (坎)** — The Abysmal / Water / The Gorge / Danger / The Pit. The judgment reads: "The Abysmal repeated. If you are sincere, you have success in your heart, and whatever you do succeeds." Kan describes danger, depth, the abyss — water flowing through a gorge, danger upon danger. But crucially, sincerity (xin 信) in the midst of danger leads to success. The Daxiang says: "Water flows on and reaches the goal: the image of the Abysmal repeated. Thus the superior man walks in lasting virtue and carries on the business of teaching." This is danger navigated through constancy.

**Li (離)** — The Clinging / Fire / Radiance / Clarity. The judgment reads: "The Clinging. Perseverance furthers. It brings success. Care of the cow brings good fortune." Li describes clarity, illumination, attachment — fire that clings to fuel, light that illuminates. But it also describes *dependence* — fire cannot exist without something to cling to. The Daxiang says: "That which is bright rises twice: the image of Fire. Thus the great man, by perpetuating this brightness, illuminates the four quarters of the world." This is clarity sustained through proper attachment.

### Directionality: Kan → Li (suggestive, but genuinely complex)

This is the most difficult pair to assess because Kan and Li occupy a cosmological position as the fundamental Water/Fire polarity — they are the "post-heaven" equivalents of Qian/Kun (Creative/Receptive), representing the active operative principles of the manifest world. As such, they might be genuinely co-equal, with no natural ordering.

Arguments for Kan first:
1. **The Yi's principle of danger before clarity:** You must pass through the abyss before you can see the light. The spiritual journey goes through darkness to reach illumination. Kan = the trial; Li = the attainment.
2. **The Xugua's logic** (see below): the sequence of the text places danger before clarity.
3. **Water before fire in cosmological ordering:** Water (associated with the north, winter, midnight) precedes fire (south, summer, noon) in the generative cycle of the year.

Arguments for Li first:
1. Fire/light could be seen as the primary creative force from which water/dark is derived.
2. The Fuxi (xiantian) arrangement places Li/Kan in positions that don't obviously privilege one over the other.

Arguments for co-equality:
1. Kan and Li are the only pair besides Qian/Kun that are formed by doubling a trigram. They occupy a uniquely balanced, cosmological position.
2. The tradition frequently treats them as co-equal poles: "Water and Fire do not shoot past each other" (水火不相射) from the Shuogua.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua marks the transition between the upper and lower canons at this pair. It states: "坎者陷也。陷必有所麗，故受之以離。離者麗也。" — "Kan means falling in / sinking. What sinks must have something to cling to; therefore Li follows. Li means clinging/attaching."

The Xugua frames Li as consequent upon Kan: having fallen into the abyss, you must find something to cling to. Danger creates the need for attachment/clarity. This is a condition → response sequence.

### Activity / Receptivity

This is genuinely complex. Both Kan and Li have both active and receptive qualities.

**Kan** — Water flows (active) but always downward (yielding to gravity). It takes the shape of its container (receptive) but is unstoppable in its course (active). Danger is something you *face* — it is an active confrontation with the world's hazards.

**Li** — Fire illuminates (active) but clings to fuel (receptive/dependent). Clarity is something you *perceive* — it is a receptive act of seeing. The cow in the judgment symbolizes docile receptivity.

If I had to assign: Kan is more *dynamic* (flowing, dangerous, challenging), Li is more *receptive* (clinging, illuminating, perceiving). But this assignment is genuinely uncertain. The tradition treats both as having both qualities.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Song-dynasty tradition treats Kan/Li as parallel to Qian/Kun — the fundamental polarity. Just as Qian precedes Kun, Kan precedes Li. This may reflect a cosmological convention (the dark/dangerous/watery principle is placed first as the *substance* to which the bright/clarifying/fiery principle then attaches), but it is a convention more than an argument.

The Zagua does not pair Kan/Li together in its otherwise pairwise structure — it treats them separately, suggesting they may occupy a structurally distinct position. However, the Zagua also treats Qian/Kun separately. This special treatment reinforces their status as cosmological anchors rather than a developmental pair.

My personal reading from the Yijing tradition (drawing on my prior study): Kan and Li are the pair that marks the hinge between the upper and lower canons. Kan closes the upper canon and Li opens the lower — danger at the end, clarity at the new beginning. But this is a structural observation about the sequence position, not a meaning-derived ordering rationale.

### Confidence: **Suggestive**

The Xugua provides a directional logic (sinking → clinging), and the broader tradition consistently places Kan before Li. But the case is weaker than for the developmental pairs. Kan/Li are more like cosmological poles than a condition → consequence sequence. The ordering is traditional and defensible but not as deeply rooted in the hexagram meanings as the other pairs assessed.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Kan first** (weaker than other predictions)

The Xugua's logic (danger necessitates attachment), the cosmological convention (water before fire), and the analogy with Qian/Kun all point to Kan first. But I would not rate this as "Clear" — the pair is genuinely closer to co-equal than the others.

### Emerging Patterns

**Round 1 found a consistent "active first" pattern.** The three M-rule exception pairs all placed the more active, outward-directed member first: Shi→Bi, Tai→Pi, Lin→Guan.

**Round 2 reveals a more complex picture.** The "active first" pattern holds for some pairs (Ge/Ding, Zhen/Gen) but *reverses* for others (Dun/Da Zhuang, Jian/Xie, Bo/Fu), where the *receptive/suffering* member comes first and the *active/resolving* member follows.

**The deeper principle is not "active first" but "condition first, consequence second."** The KW ordering consistently places the *initiating condition* before its *natural consequence*:

| Logic | Pairs |
|-------|-------|
| Action → Response | Shi→Bi, Tai→Pi, Lin→Guan, Ge→Ding, Zhen→Gen |
| Problem → Resolution | Jian→Xie, Bo→Fu, Kun→Jing |
| Discipline → Growth | Sun→Yi |
| Withdrawal → Advance | Dun→Da Zhuang |
| Danger → Clarity | Kan→Li |

The unifying principle is **developmental priority**: whatever creates the conditions for the other member comes first, regardless of whether that initiating condition is active (Revolution, Thunder) or receptive (Oppression, Retreat, Splitting Apart).

### Falsification Note

All 8 predictions aligned. Combined with Round 1's 3/3, that is 11/11. The probability of this by chance (11 heads in 11 coin flips) is p = 0.000488 (1 in 2048). The null hypothesis of random alignment is becoming untenable.

However, I must note a potential confound: **the Xugua itself may be the source of the ordering, not independent meaning-level analysis.** If the Xugua was written *to rationalize* the existing KW ordering, then my readings are partially circular — I'm reading the Xugua's explanations and finding they match the KW ordering, which is what the Xugua was designed to do. The stronger test is whether the hexagram meanings *independent of the Xugua* predict the ordering. In most cases above (especially Jian/Xie, Ge/Ding, Bo/Fu, Zhen/Gen, Tai/Pi), the answer is yes — the ordering is overdetermined by the meanings themselves, not just by the Xugua's rationalization. But for pairs like Dun/Da Zhuang and Kan/Li, the Xugua's logic is doing more of the work.

### Where meaning is weakest

The three "Suggestive" ratings (Dun/Da Zhuang, Kun/Jing, Kan/Li) are the pairs where a reversed ordering could also be defended on meaning grounds. These are the boundary cases that might reveal where algebra overrides meaning, or where meaning is genuinely silent and other principles fill the gap.
