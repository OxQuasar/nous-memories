# Round 1: Reader Assessment — Three M-Rule Exception Pairs

Reading the traditional texts for three hexagram pairs. Assessment based purely on meanings, names, judgments (卦辭), images (象辭), Xugua (序卦傳), Zagua (雜卦), and the broader commentarial tradition. No reference to line structures, binary values, or algebraic properties.

---

## Pair A: Shi 師 (#7, The Army) / Bi 比 (#8, Holding Together)

**KW order: Shi first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Shi (師)** — The Army. The character 師 means both "army" and "teacher/multitude." The judgment reads: "The Army. Perseverance. An experienced man. Good fortune. No blame." The situation is one of disciplined collective action — mobilizing many people under one strong leader for a common purpose. It describes the phase of marshaling, organizing, and directing collective force. The Daxiang says: "In the midst of the earth, there is water: the image of The Army. Thus the superior man increases his masses by generosity toward the people." This is a situation of active mobilization.

**Bi (比)** — Holding Together / Union / Alliance. The judgment reads: "Holding Together brings good fortune. Inquire of the oracle once again whether you possess sublimity, constancy, and perseverance; then there is no blame. Those who are uncertain gradually join. Whoever comes too late meets with misfortune." This describes the phase of bonding, alliance, voluntary affiliation. Where Shi commands, Bi attracts. Where Shi organizes by authority, Bi unites by affinity. The Daxiang says: "On the earth there is water: the image of Holding Together. Thus the kings of antiquity bestowed the different states as fiefs and cultivated friendly relations with the feudal lords."

### Directionality: Shi → Bi (clear)

The traditional reading presents a natural developmental sequence: first you gather the multitude (Shi), then the multitude forms bonds of union (Bi). Mobilization precedes alliance. You cannot have voluntary holding-together without first having a gathered multitude — the Army creates the collective, and Holding Together gives it coherent social form. This is a condition → consequence sequence.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "師者眾也。眾必有所比，故受之以比。" — "Shi has the signification of multitudes, and between multitudes there must be some bond of union. Hence it is followed by Bi." This is an explicit causal-developmental claim: the existence of multitudes (produced by the Army situation) necessitates bonds of union (the Bi situation). The Xugua's logic is: gathering creates the need for affiliation.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Shi is more active and outward-directed.** It describes mobilization, command, the exertion of authority over a multitude. It is the phase of action — marshaling, campaigning, directing collective force outward.

**Bi is more receptive and inward-directed.** It describes the yielding-together, the coming-into-relationship. Its mode is attraction rather than command. People "gradually join." The latecomer suffers — the window is open, not forced. Bi is about receiving affiliation, not compelling it.

The Zagua confirms this distinction: "Bi is joy, Shi is sadness/anxiety (比樂師憂)." The Army carries the weight of responsibility and danger; Holding Together carries the satisfaction of belonging. Anxiety is the emotion of the active mobilizer; joy is the emotion of the receptively united.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The commentarial tradition consistently treats this pair as a developmental sequence from collective mobilization to collective unity. Cheng Yi's commentary follows the Xugua's logic closely — where there are multitudes, there must be bonds. The Zagua's characterization of Shi as "anxiety" and Bi as "joy" also suggests a before/after — the stressful gathering followed by the joyful unity. The pair models the progression from military organization to political harmony, a theme deeply resonant with Zhou political philosophy.

### Confidence: **Clear**

The texts unambiguously present Shi → Bi as a developmental sequence: mobilization precedes union.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Shi first**

The more active, outward-directed, mobilizing hexagram (Shi) naturally precedes the more receptive, inward-directed, bonding hexagram (Bi). The Xugua, Zagua, and broader tradition all support this ordering.

## Pair B: Tai 泰 (#11, Peace) / Pi 否 (#12, Standstill)

**KW order: Tai first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Tai (泰)** — Peace / Pervading / Flow. The judgment reads: "Peace. The small departs, the great approaches. Good fortune. Success." Tai describes the condition of complete communion between heaven and earth — everything flows together, creative energy moves freely, communication is open. It is the moment of maximal flourishing, where inner strength meets outer receptivity and all things prosper. The Daxiang says the ruler "fashions and completes the course of heaven and earth... and so aids the people."

**Pi (否)** — Standstill / Obstruction / Stagnation. The judgment reads: "Standstill. Evil people do not further the perseverance of the superior man. The great departs, the small approaches." Pi is the exact inversion of Tai's situation: communication ceases, creative and receptive forces separate, nothing flows. The superior man withdraws; the mean-spirited advance. The Daxiang says the superior man "falls back upon his inner worth in order to escape the difficulties. He does not permit himself to be honored with revenue."

### Directionality: Tai → Pi (clear, with cosmological inevitability)

This is the paradigmatic pair of cyclical succession in the Yijing. The traditional reading is absolutely unambiguous: Peace naturally gives way to Standstill. Tai represents the apex of flourishing, but the Yi's fundamental principle is that when a condition reaches its fullness, it transforms into its opposite. "The small departs, the great approaches" (Tai) necessarily precedes "The great departs, the small approaches" (Pi). Prosperity invites decline. This is not merely a sequence — it is a cosmological law.

However, the cycle also runs the other way (Pi → Tai, standstill eventually gives way to peace). So why Tai first?

The answer from the tradition: **the generative/flourishing condition is prior**. Peace is the natural, intended state of the cosmos — when heaven and earth are in communion, that is the "right" ordering. Standstill is the disruption, the deviation. You begin with the ideal and then show its dissolution. This matches the broader pattern of the early hexagrams: Qian/Kun → Zhun/Meng → ... each pair begins with the more primal or generative condition.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua for this transition reads: "物不可以終通，故受之以否。" — "Things cannot forever flow together, and so Standstill follows." (The Xugua commentary attributed in the tradition.) This explicitly frames Pi as the *consequence* of Tai — not the other way around. Tai is the condition; Pi is what inevitably follows when that condition exhausts itself.

### Activity / Receptivity

This pair is more nuanced than Pair A.

**Tai is more active and dynamic.** Despite being called "Peace," Tai is a hexagram of intense exchange and communication — everything is moving, creative force is rising, receptive force is descending, they meet in the middle. It is the moment of maximal dynamism. As one commentator notes, Tai "is a hexagram of intense dynamism" — not passive peace but active, flowing interchange.

**Pi is more inward and withdrawn.** The superior man retreats inward. Nothing moves outward with effect. It is the hexagram of strategic withdrawal, conservation, and waiting. The mode is receptive endurance rather than active creation.

So Tai = active/outward, Pi = receptive/inward — and Tai comes first.

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Tai/Pi pair is one of the most discussed in the entire commentarial tradition. Virtually every major commentator (Wang Bi, Cheng Yi, Zhu Xi) treats this as the paradigmatic example of cyclic succession: flourishing → decline. The Zagua says "Tai and Pi are their reversals (反其類也)" — they are of opposite kind. But the tradition consistently places the positive/flourishing/active condition first.

The Tuan (Commentary on the Judgment) for Tai explicitly describes heaven and earth in communion, the myriad things flowing together. For Pi, it describes their separation. Creation before dissolution; communion before separation.

### Confidence: **Clear**

The most thoroughly discussed pair in the tradition. Tai → Pi is universally treated as the natural order.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Tai first**

The active, flourishing, communicative hexagram (Tai) naturally precedes the withdrawn, stagnant, receptive hexagram (Pi). Every layer of the tradition confirms this.

## Pair C: Lin 臨 (#19, Approach) / Guan 觀 (#20, Contemplation)

**KW order: Lin first**

### The Hexagrams as Situations

**Lin (臨)** — Approach / Nearing / Overseeing. The judgment reads: "Approach has supreme success. Perseverance furthers. When the eighth month comes, there will be misfortune." Lin describes the situation of drawing near — specifically, the approach of greatness, of strength, of authority moving toward engagement. The character 臨 implies looking down from above, overseeing, the ruler approaching his subjects. It can also mean the approach of a favorable time. The Daxiang says: "The superior man is inexhaustible in his will to teach, and without limits in his toleration and protection of the people." This is a posture of active engagement — going toward, overseeing, governing.

**Guan (觀)** — Contemplation / Viewing / Observation. The judgment reads: "Contemplation. The ablution has been made, but not yet the offering. Full of trust they look up to him." Guan describes the situation of seeing, observing, taking in — specifically, being *seen* and *seeing from above*. The character 觀 has a double meaning: to view (active observation) and to be viewed (becoming an example for others). The Daxiang says: "The wind blows over the earth: the image of Contemplation. Thus the kings of old visited the regions of the world, contemplated the people, and gave them instruction." This is a more detached, receptive posture — observing rather than intervening, understanding before acting.

### Directionality: Lin → Guan (clear)

The traditional reading treats these as a developmental pair: first you approach (draw near, engage, oversee), then you contemplate (observe, understand, take the wider view). Lin is the active phase of engagement; Guan is the reflective phase of understanding. The Zagua says: "Lin and Guan have the meaning that sometimes one gives and sometimes one seeks (臨觀之義，或與或求)." This "giving and seeking" maps onto approach (giving, extending oneself outward) and contemplation (seeking, drawing understanding inward).

There is also a temporal/seasonal reading: Lin corresponds to the approach of the growing season (the twelfth month, when yang begins to grow), while Guan corresponds to the observation of what has developed (the eighth month, when yin begins to advance). In the annual cycle, the approach of growth precedes the contemplation of results.

### Xugua (Sequence Commentary)

The Xugua states: "以大者臨必有可觀，故受之以觀。" — "When one approaches with greatness, there will be something worth contemplating; therefore Contemplation follows." (The exact phrasing: those who have something great to oversee will inevitably become worth viewing/contemplating.) This is an explicit causal claim: approaching/overseeing generates something worth contemplating. Lin creates the conditions for Guan.

After Guan, the Xugua continues: "可觀而後有所合" — "After there is something worth contemplating, there will be union" — leading to Hexagram 21 (Shi He). This further confirms Guan as the second member, the result of Lin's approach.

### Activity / Receptivity

**Lin is more active and outward-directed.** It describes approaching, drawing near, overseeing — the ruler extends himself toward the governed. The posture is one of active engagement, going forth, exercising authority. "Looking down" at what one governs.

**Guan is more receptive and inward-directed.** It describes contemplation, observation — taking in what is there, understanding the situation. Even when the ruler "contemplates the people," the primary action is reception of information, not projection of force. "Looking up" to understand. The character's association with Guan Yin (the bodhisattva who *hears/observes* the cries of the world) captures the receptive quality.

The Zagua's formulation "or giving, or seeking" maps perfectly: Lin = giving (active, projecting), Guan = seeking (receptive, absorbing).

### Traditional Ordering Rationale

The Zagua specifically pairs these two and characterizes their relationship as complementary: "The meaning of Lin and Guan is that sometimes one gives and sometimes one seeks." The Shaughnessy/Brill translation renders Lin as "Looking Down" and Guan as "Looking Up" — the ruler first looks down (actively governs/oversees) and then looks up (reflectively contemplates the larger pattern). The tradition treats this as a natural action → reflection sequence.

Cheng Yi's commentary treats Lin as the phase of the ruler's active approach and Guan as the phase where the effects of that approach become visible and contemplable. The seasonal correspondence (Lin = 12th month/growing yang; Guan = 8th month/growing yin) reinforces the sense that the active/expanding phase precedes the contemplative/contracting phase.

### Confidence: **Clear**

The Xugua's causal claim, the Zagua's "giving and seeking" characterization, and the seasonal correspondences all consistently support Lin → Guan.

### Meaning-predicted order: **Lin first**

The active, approaching, outward-directed hexagram (Lin) naturally precedes the contemplative, observing, inward-directed hexagram (Guan). The Xugua, Zagua, and broader tradition all confirm this.

## Summary

| Pair | KW Order | Meaning-Predicted Order | Active Member | Receptive Member | Confidence |
|------|----------|------------------------|---------------|------------------|------------|
| A: Shi/Bi | Shi first | **Shi first** | Shi (mobilizing) | Bi (bonding) | Clear |
| B: Tai/Pi | Tai first | **Tai first** | Tai (flowing) | Pi (withdrawing) | Clear |
| C: Lin/Guan | Lin first | **Lin first** | Lin (approaching) | Guan (contemplating) | Clear |

**Consistent pattern across all three pairs:** The KW ordering places the more active, outward-directed, initiating hexagram first and the more receptive, inward-directed, responsive hexagram second. The traditional texts (Xugua, Zagua, and broader commentary) unanimously support this ordering in all three cases.

In each pair, the first member describes a situation of active engagement with the world (mobilizing an army, cosmic communion in full flow, the ruler approaching his domain), and the second describes a more receptive response (forming bonds, withdrawing inward, contemplating what one has seen). The sequence is consistently: action → response, projection → reception, engagement → reflection.
