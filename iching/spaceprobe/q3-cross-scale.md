# Q3: Cross-Scale Involution Structure — n=3 vs n=6

## 1. The Question

At n=3, three independent fixed-point-free involutions on 8 trigrams generate S₄ acting on 4 blocks of 2. The tradition chose complement (ι₁) as the pairing — a pure group element.

At n=6, the tradition chose a hybrid pairing (reversal where possible, complement for palindromes) that produces the KW hexagram pairs. The opposition theory showed this is one of exactly 9 equivariant pairings under a 384-element symmetry group.

**Q3 asks:** What is the analogous involution structure at n=6? How does it compare to n=3? Does the group theory explain the cross-scale divergence (complement at n=3, reversal-hybrid at n=6)?

---

## 2. The n=3 Structure (from prior work)

### Generators and group

Three fixed-point-free involutions on 8 trigrams:
- **ι₁** (Fu Xi complement): XOR(111), all pairs at Hamming 3
- **ι₂** (KW/Lo Shu diametric): XOR masks {001, 010, 100, 111}, max diversity
- **ι₃** (He Tu): XOR masks {101, 110}, uniform Hamming 2

All three are XOR translations — the same algebraic species. They generate G = ⟨ι₁, ι₂, ι₃⟩ ≅ S₄ (order 24) acting faithfully on 4 blocks of 2: {Kun,Zhen}, {Gen,Dui}, {Kan,Li}, {Xun,Qian}.

### Internal structure

V₄ = ⟨ι₂, ι₃⟩ ◁ S₄, with quotient S₃. The fourth involution τ = ι₂∘ι₃ pairs elements within blocks. Product-order fingerprint: ord(ι₁∘ι₂) = 3, ord(ι₁∘ι₃) = 4, ord(ι₂∘ι₃) = 2.

### Characterization

Two axioms — overlap pattern (1,0,0) and commutation (ι₂∘ι₃ has order 2) — uniquely determine the structure up to a single orientation bit (Aut = Z₂).

### Traditional pairing

ι₁ (complement) IS a group element of ⟨ι₁, ι₂, ι₃⟩. It acts uniformly by a single XOR mask on all 8 elements. No fixed points (no 3-bit string is self-complementary since 3 is odd).

## 3. The n=6 Structure (this investigation)

### The basic involutions

Three involutions on 64 hexagrams:
- **σ₁** (complement): h → h ⊕ 111111. Order 2, 0 fixed points, 32 two-cycles.
- **σ₂** (reversal): h → reverse bit string (L1↔L6, L2↔L5, L3↔L4). Order 2, **8 fixed points** (palindromes), 28 two-cycles.
- **σ₃** (comp∘rev): σ₁∘σ₂. Order 2, **8 fixed points** (anti-palindromes), 28 two-cycles.

**All three commute.** σ₁ flips values; σ₂ permutes positions. These operations are independent, so G₀ = ⟨σ₁, σ₂⟩ ≅ V₄ (Klein four-group, order 4).

This is structurally trivial — V₄ is forced whenever complement and reversal are combined at any bit length. It is NOT the n=6 analogue of the n=3 S₄.

### Fixed points: the even-length phenomenon

At n=3 (odd length): σ₂ has 4 fixed points (palindromic trigrams: 000, 010, 101, 111), but σ₃ has **0** fixed points (no 3-bit string satisfies L_i + L_{4-i} = 1 for all i, because the center bit would need to be its own complement).

At n=6 (even length): both σ₂ and σ₃ have 8 fixed points each. The 8 palindromes satisfy L_i = L_{7-i} for all i. The 8 anti-palindromes satisfy L_i + L_{7-i} = 1 for all i. Both exist because even length permits full mirror symmetry and anti-symmetry.

### The mirror-pair translation group T

The key structural move: identify the **mirror-pair XOR masks** as the natural translation subgroup.

The 6 line positions form 3 mirror pairs: {L1,L6}, {L2,L5}, {L3,L4}. Define:
- **O** (outer): XOR mask flipping L1 and L6 = 0b100001 = 33
- **M** (middle): XOR mask flipping L2 and L5 = 0b010010 = 18
- **I** (inner): XOR mask flipping L3 and L4 = 0b001100 = 12

These generate T = ⟨O, M, I⟩ ≅ Z₂³ (order 8), a subgroup of the full translation group Z₂⁶. Note: O ⊕ M ⊕ I = complement = σ₁.

**T acts freely on {0,...,63}**, producing **8 orbits of size 8**. The orbits are cosets of T in Z₂⁶, indexed by the **mirror-pair residual**:

φ(h) = (L1⊕L6, L2⊕L5, L3⊕L4) ∈ Z₂³

Each residual bit records whether a mirror pair is "aligned" (0) or "crossed" (1):

| Residual | Weight | Meaning | Macro-orbit |
|----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| 000 | 0 | All pairs same → palindromes | A (size 8) |
| 001, 010, 100 | 1 | One pair crossed | C (size 24) |
| 011, 101, 110 | 2 | Two pairs crossed | D (size 24) |
| 111 | 3 | All pairs crossed → anti-palindromes | B (size 8) |

The quotient Z₂⁶/T ≅ Z₂³ is a copy of the trigram space — not the upper or lower trigram, but the **mirror-pair difference signature**.

### The 384-element group G₃₈₄

G₃₈₄ is generated by three layers of operations:

1. **Pair-value flips** (T = ⟨O, M, I⟩ ≅ Z₂³): XOR masks on mirror pairs
2. **Within-pair position swaps** (⟨swap L1↔L6, swap L2↔L5, swap L3↔L4⟩ ≅ Z₂³): exchange positions within each mirror pair
3. **Pair permutations** (⟨swap_OM, cycle_OMI⟩ ≅ S₃): permute the 3 mirror pairs as units

Layers 1 and 2 commute, giving K = T × ⟨swaps⟩ ≅ Z₂⁶ (order 64). Layer 3 acts on K by conjugation, permuting pair labels. The full group:

G₃₈₄ = K ⋊ S₃ = (Z₂ ≀ S₃) × Z₂³, order 384

**Short exact sequence:** 1 → K ≅ Z₂⁶ → G₃₈₄ → S₃ → 1

K is normal. K preserves all T-orbits (acts "within orbit"). S₃ permutes T-orbits by permuting the residual coordinates.

**Key members:**
- σ₁ (complement) = O·M·I ∈ T ⊂ K — a group element
- σ₂ (reversal) = swap_L1L6 · swap_L2L5 · swap_L3L4 ∈ K — a group element
- σ₃ (comp∘rev) = σ₁·σ₂ ∈ K — a group element

All three basic involutions live in the kernel K. None of them act on the T-orbit quotient.

### The 4 macro-orbits

S₃ acts on the 8 T-orbits (indexed by 3-bit residuals) by permuting coordinates. This produces 4 orbits of the quotient action, determined by Hamming weight of the residual:

| Macro-orbit | Residual weight | T-orbits merged | Total size | Pairing forced? |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|------------|-----------------|
| A | 0 | 1 (res=000) | 8 | Yes (palindromes → complement) |
| B | 3 | 1 (res=111) | 8 | Yes (anti-palindromes → rev=comp) |
| C | 1 | 3 (res=001,010,100) | 24 | No (3 choices: rev, comp, comp∘rev) |
| D | 2 | 3 (res=011,101,110) | 24 | No (3 choices: rev, comp, comp∘rev) |

Equivariance under G₃₈₄ forces the same operation within each macro-orbit. Orbits A and B are forced. Orbits C and D each have 3 independent choices. Total: **1 × 1 × 3 × 3 = 9 equivariant pairings**.

## 5. KW ∉ G₃₈₄

**The KW pairing is not a group element.**

The KW rule: "reverse the hexagram; if the result is itself (palindrome), complement instead." This applies σ₂ on 48 hexagrams and σ₁ on 16 (the 8 palindromes and their 8 complements). A group element must act by a single algebraic operation uniformly. The KW hybrid selects between operations based on a property — palindromicity — that the group itself defines but does not use for selection.

Verified computationally: `G_384.contains(kw_permutation)` returns False.

The three pure involutions σ₁, σ₂, σ₃ ARE all in G₃₈₄ (they live in K). But none of them is the KW pairing. σ₂ has 8 fixed points (palindromes). σ₁ would pair everything by complement (WT = 1.875, pairing #5). σ₃ has 8 fixed points (anti-palindromes).

**The KW pairing is structurally inevitable as the unique FPF hybrid.** To build a fixed-point-free involution from {σ₁, σ₂, σ₃}: σ₁ is already FPF, so pairing #5 (all complement) works. But to get weight preservation, you need σ₂ (reversal) — which has fixed points. The only way to "fix" σ₂ into an FPF involution using the available group elements is to substitute σ₁ on the fixed-point set. This gives exactly KW. The hybrid is forced by the conjunction of weight preservation and fixed-point-freedom.

**Algebraic status.** The KW pairing is equivariant *under* G₃₈₄ without being *in* G₃₈₄. It lives in the normalizer of the macro-orbit decomposition in Sym(64), respecting the group's constraint structure without being a group element. The "grammar vs. sentence" relationship: the group provides the vocabulary (the 3 involutions) and the grammar (equivariance constraining to 9 pairings); KW is a well-formed sentence composed from those elements but not itself a word.

## 6. Cross-Scale Comparison

| Property | n=3 (trigrams) | n=6 (hexagrams) |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **State space** | Z₂³ (8 elements) | Z₂⁶ (64 elements) |
| **Mirror-pair XOR group T** | Z₂³ acting on itself (regular rep) | Z₂³ as subgroup of Z₂⁶ (index 8) |
| **T-orbits** | 1 (whole space) | 8 (size 8 each) |
| **Quotient space** | trivial | Z₂⁶/T ≅ Z₂³ (residual) |
| **Quotient action** | S₄ on 4 blocks of 2 | S₃ on 8 residuals → 4 macro-orbits |
| **Macro-orbits** | 4 blocks of 2 | 4 orbits (8, 8, 24, 24) |
| **Kernel** | V₄ ◁ S₄ | K ≅ Z₂⁶ ◁ G₃₈₄ |
| **Kernel quotient** | S₃ | S₃ |
| **Equivariant pairings** | 9 | 9 |
| **Why 9?** | 2 free blocks × 3 choices = 3² | 2 free macro-orbits × 3 choices = 3² |
| **Traditional pairing** | ι₁ (complement) | KW (rev + comp hybrid) |
| **Pairing ∈ group?** | **Yes** (ι₁ ∈ Z₂³) | **No** (KW ∉ G₃₈₄) |
| **Pairing criterion** | Max strength (S) | Min weight tilt (WT) |

### What is shared: the counting mechanism

Both scales produce exactly 9 equivariant pairings for the same structural reason. The symmetry group forces a block/macro-orbit decomposition with exactly 2 "free" components. Each free component admits 3 involution choices (reversal, complement, comp∘rev). The pairing count is 3² = 9 regardless of scale.

The S₃ quotient appears at both scales, permuting the structural coordinates. At n=3, S₃ = G/V₄ acts on the 4 blocks. At n=6, S₃ = G₃₈₄/K acts on the 8 T-orbits (merging them into 4 weight classes).

### What differs: the nature of the traditional pairing

At n=3, the traditional pairing (complement ι₁) is a pure group element — a single XOR translation applied uniformly to all 8 trigrams. It has no fixed points because n is odd (no 3-bit string is self-complementary). It maximizes opposition strength (S). Crucially, at n=3 complement is *simultaneously* the strongest pairing, a group element, and weight-agnostic (all pairings have comparable weight tilt). There is no tradeoff — all desirable properties align.

At n=6, the traditional pairing (KW) is NOT a group element. It is a conditional hybrid: reversal on the 48 non-palindromic hexagrams, complement on the 16 palindromic ones. It exits the group because even length creates palindromic fixed points that force the hybrid construction. It minimizes weight tilt (WT) rather than maximizing strength. Here, group-membership and weight-preservation *diverge* — they cannot be simultaneously achieved.

### The structural reason for the divergence

**The divergence is not "same tradeoff, different resolution." It is "no tradeoff at n=3 → tradeoff emerges at n=6."** The parity boundary creates a new degree of tension that did not exist before.

At odd n, complement is FPF, a group element, and the strongest involution. Nothing competes. At even n, the weight-optimal operation (reversal) develops fixed points, forcing a hybrid that exits the group. The tradition must choose: algebraic purity (stay in the group → all complement, pairing #5, WT = 1.875) or weight preservation (exit the group → KW hybrid, WT = 0.375). It chooses weight preservation.

The deeper fork: at n=3, with only 1 mirror pair, complement and reversal are not structurally distinguishable in opposition measure — both are valid and complement is stronger. At n=6, with 3 mirror pairs, the mirror-pair geometry is rich enough to differentiate them sharply, and weight preservation (which only reversal provides) becomes the selecting criterion.

## 7. The Missing Masks

At n=3, the single missing mask was 011 (= e₂⊕e₃), the only nonzero element of Z₂³ unused by any involution pair. This was flagged as an open question.

At n=6, the mirror-pair mask space has 7 nonzero elements. **KW uses all 7** — there is no missing mask. The complement mask (OMI = 111111) appears in 8 of 32 pairs; the other 6 masks appear in 4 pairs each.

This is a consequence of the n=6 structure: KW needs reversal masks for the non-palindromic pairs (6 distinct reversal masks covering all single-pair, double-pair, and triple-pair crossings) plus the complement mask for palindromes. The mask vocabulary is saturated.

Among the other 8 pairings, mask usage varies: the Shao Yong complement pairing (#5) uses only 1 mask (111111); the mixed pairings use 4 masks; only KW and its mirror (#9) use all 7.

## 8. The Phase Transition and the Structural Argument for n=6

KW-style pairing (reversal where possible, complement where forced) is mediocre at n=4 (75th percentile in opposition strength) but extreme at n=6 (99.98th percentile). The group structure explains this.

**At n=4** (2 mirror pairs): The stabilizer group is smaller and the orbit structure less constraining. The quotient action involves S₂ on the mirror-pair residual — too weak to produce the tight 4-macro-orbit structure. 117 equivariant pairings exist (vs 9 at n=6). KW-style is not distinguished.

**At n=6** (3 mirror pairs): Three independent mirror pairs generate Z₂³, which acts freely on Z₂⁶ with 8 orbits. The S₃ pair-permutation group merges these into 4 macro-orbits with the precise structure (2 forced, 2 free) that yields 9 pairings. The threefold gap in weight tilt (0.375 vs 1.125) uniquely selects KW.

**At n=8** (4 mirror pairs): S₄ would act on the quotient Z₂⁴ — a *different* structure with different macro-orbit counts. The tight 2-forced/2-free decomposition specific to n=6 would not recur in the same form.

**The threshold is 3 mirror pairs.** This is the minimum number needed for S₃ to act non-trivially on the quotient Z₂³, creating the weight-class stratification (weight 0, 1, 2, 3 of the residual) that produces exactly 2 forced + 2 free macro-orbits. Below this threshold, the structure is too sparse to constrain; at and above it, the structure changes character.

**n=6 is the minimum even length where the constraint space is tight.** At n=2 (1 mirror pair), the structure is trivial. At n=4 (2 pairs), 117 pairings — too many for unique selection. At n=6 (3 pairs), exactly 9 pairings with a unique WT-minimizer. This gives a structural argument for the hexagram length: 6 is the smallest n where mirror-pair geometry produces a sharp, unique selection.

## 9. What This Resolves from the Prior Open Questions

### From invariants.md §10:

**Q3 (n=6 triple-involution analysis):** Answered. The n=3 characterization does NOT have a direct analog at n=6. The n=3 S₄ arose from three FPF involutions (all XOR translations); at n=6, complement and reversal generate only V₄ (trivially forced). The structural analog is the 384-element group acting on the mirror-pair residual space, with S₃ quotient.

**Q6 (cross-scale bridge):** Partially resolved. The divergence between complement (n=3) and reversal-hybrid (n=6) is structurally forced by parity and mirror-pair count. The common structural feature is the 3² = 9 counting mechanism. A single principle that *generates* both choices from the same source was not found — the divergence is an irreducible geometric fork between regimes where no tradeoff exists (n=3: complement is simultaneously the strongest, a group element, and weight-neutral) and where a new tradeoff emerges (n=6: weight preservation requires exiting the group). The fork is between the absence and the presence of structural tension, not between two resolutions of the same tension.

### From the opposition theory synthesis:

**The 9-pairing derivation:** Fully reproduced from the group-theoretic framework. The 9 pairings arise as the independent product of 3 choices on 2 free macro-orbits. The measures (S, WT, D) computed for all 9 confirm KW as the unique WT-minimizer.

**KW's hybrid nature:** The KW pairing's non-membership in G₃₈₄ is now established. The opposition theory noted that "KW uses reversal wherever possible" — Q3 adds: this hybrid construction is structurally forced by the conjunction of weight optimization and fixed-point-freedom, and it is the unique such hybrid.

## 10. Summary

The n=3 and n=6 involution structures share a common skeleton: a translation subgroup T ≅ Z₂³ whose orbits are organized by an S₃ quotient action into macro-orbits, yielding 3² = 9 equivariant pairings. The tradition selects one pairing at each scale: complement at n=3, the reversal-hybrid at n=6.

The cross-scale divergence is not a design inconsistency but a geometric necessity. At n=3, no tradeoff exists — complement is simultaneously the strongest pairing, a group element, and the natural choice. At n=6, parity creates palindromic fixed points, forcing a tradeoff between algebraic purity and weight preservation that did not exist at n=3. The tradition resolves this new tension by choosing weight preservation, producing a hybrid that exits the group.

The group does not make the choice. It constrains the space of choices from astronomical (~10¹⁷ arbitrary pairings at n=6) to 9. The tradition's selection — KW, uniquely minimizing weight tilt — lies outside the group that defines it. The group provides the vocabulary and grammar; the KW pairing is a sentence composed from group elements but not itself one.

n=6 is the minimum even length where this constraint space is tight (3 mirror pairs → S₃ → exactly 9 pairings → unique WT-minimizer). This gives a structural argument for the hexagram length itself.

## 11. Open Questions

1. **The n=8 structure.** With 4 mirror pairs, S₄ acts on the quotient Z₂⁴. What is the macro-orbit decomposition? How many equivariant pairings exist? Does the KW-style pairing remain distinguished?

2. **Piecewise group actions.** KW is composed of group elements applied conditionally on macro-orbits. Is there a natural algebraic framework (e.g., a category of equivariant piecewise involutions) in which KW becomes a first-class object rather than a hybrid?

3. **The center line at odd n.** At n=3, the unpaired center bit L₂ makes complement decompose as mirror-mask ⊕ center-flip (101 ⊕ 010 = 111). At n=5, the center bit creates the lexicographic complication (WT-minimization needs S as tiebreaker). Is there a unified account of how center bits modulate the constraint structure?

4. **The polarity partition at n=6.** The n=3 investigation found that the polarity partition P₊/P₋ requires an external orientation axiom. Does the n=6 structure have an analogous orientation question, and what resolves it?
