# Round 2: Testing Whether the Grammar is Real — Results

## Headline: The MI is NOT real. But OMI dominance in deltas IS.

### 1. MI null distribution (the critical test)

| Metric | KW | Null mean | Null std | KW percentile |
|--------|-----|-----------|----------|---------------|
| MI (unconditional) | 1.331 | 1.141 | 0.168 | 87.9% |
| MI (conditional on f1 ≥ 1.70) | 1.331 | 1.191 | 0.159 | 81.7% |
| MI (conditional on f1 ≥ 1.60) | 1.331 | 1.169 | 0.161 | 84.9% |

**f1 and MI are weakly correlated** (r = 0.115). High-f1 orderings have slightly elevated MI (mean 1.19 vs 1.14 overall), but not enough to explain KW's MI. However, KW's MI is still only at the **81.7th percentile** among sequences with comparable f1. This is mildly elevated but not extreme.

**Verdict:** The MI of 1.33 bits is NOT a strong signal. It's 0.43σ above the shuffle null mean (1.279). The Miller-Madow bias correction wipes out most of it (corrected MI = 0.922). The transition structure is **not significantly non-random** given the kernel type frequencies.

### 2. Second-order kernels (transition deltas) — the real finding

The transition delta sequence δᵢ = kᵢ ⊕ kᵢ₊₁ has one very strong signal:

| Delta type | KW count | Null mean ± std | KW percentile | z-score |
|------------|----------|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| id (repeat) | 2 | 3.83 ± 1.84 | 24.6% | -1.00 |
| O | 1 | 3.75 ± 1.81 | 9.8% | -1.52 |
| M | 5 | 3.75 ± 1.81 | 83.6% | +0.69 |
| I | 5 | 3.74 ± 1.81 | 83.6% | +0.70 |
| OM | 3 | 3.75 ± 1.81 | 47.3% | -0.41 |
| OI | 2 | 3.75 ± 1.81 | 25.9% | -0.96 |
| MI | 4 | 3.74 ± 1.82 | 68.2% | +0.14 |
| **OMI** | **8** | **3.69 ± 1.80** | **99.2%** | **+2.39** |

**OMI dominates the delta distribution at the 99.2nd percentile (z = +2.39).** 8 of 30 transitions are full-complement transitions (all 3 kernel bits flip). This is the "maximum opposition between consecutive transitions" signal — it's specifically about complement transitions, not about high distance in general.

The O delta (single outer-bit flip) is underrepresented at the 9.8th percentile. The pattern: **KW preferentially uses the most extreme transition (OMI = all bits flip) and avoids the minimal outer-only transition (O).**

Delta entropy: 2.774 bits (28.6th percentile — slightly low, reflecting the OMI concentration).

### 3. Running-product path — coset structure

Returns to identity occur at positions: 5, 6, 10, 11, 15, 28 (bridge numbers). Gaps: 1, 4, 1, 4, 13.

The path shows a **double-touch pattern** for identity returns: positions (4,5), (9,10), (14,—). Two of three return pairs are consecutive (gap=1), meaning the kernel at those positions is id (the running product was already at id, and the next kernel is id, leaving it there).

**Subgroup {id, O, MI, OMI} has anomalous residence: 20/31 = 64.5%** (expected 50%). This is the order-4 subgroup generated by {O, MI}. The running product spends disproportionate time in this subgroup.

Element I is reached latest (position 24) and visited least (2 times). The path has a "hole" — the I direction is underexplored.

### 4. Trigram-pair structure

- 30/32 distinct upper-trigram pairs (near-unique)
- 30/32 distinct lower-trigram pairs (near-unique)
- Upper trigram same across bridge: 4/31 (12.9%)
- Lower trigram same across bridge: 5/31 (16.1%)
- Consecutive pairs sharing ANY trigram: 20/31 (64.5%)

The trigram transition matrices are sparse and irregular — no obvious simple pattern. The sequence doesn't organize primarily by trigram continuity (only ~15% of bridges preserve a trigram), but there's modest trigram sharing between neighbors (65%).

**Notable: Pairs 21-24 all have Lake as exit upper trigram.** This is a run of 4 consecutive Lake-headed transitions — the most concentrated trigram clustering in the sequence.

### 5. Bias-corrected MI

| Measure | Value |
|---------|-------|
| MI_raw | 1.331 |
| Miller-Madow bias | -0.409 |
| MI_corrected | 0.922 |
| Shuffle null mean | 1.279 |
| Shuffle null 95th %ile | 1.483 |
| KW MI vs shuffle null | 70.4th percentile |
| Excess over shuffle | 0.052 bits (0.43σ) |

**The bias correction is massive** (0.41 bits out of 1.33). With 30 transitions and 25 nonzero cells in the 8×8 matrix, the small-sample upward bias on MI is ~31% of the raw value. After correction, KW's MI is unremarkable.

The shuffle null (which preserves marginal kernel frequencies) has mean MI = 1.279 — very close to the raw 1.331. This confirms that most of the observed MI is an artifact of the marginal frequency distribution + small-sample bias, not genuine sequential structure.

## Implications

1. **The "grammar" claim (MI = 1.33) is an artifact.** After bias correction and conditional analysis, there is no significant transition grammar in the kernel sequence. The sage's suspicion was correct.

2. **The real signal is in the delta distribution, not the transition matrix.** OMI (complement) deltas at 99.2nd percentile is strong. KW specifically maximizes full-opposition transitions — not just "high distance" generically, but specifically the all-three-bits-flip transitions.

3. **The O (outer-only flip) deficit** (9.8th percentile) is the complement of the OMI surplus. Together they describe a single phenomenon: KW's kernel sequence avoids small changes and favors maximum changes.

4. **The running product's subgroup bias** ({id, O, MI, OMI} at 64.5% residence) may be connected to the OMI delta dominance — if you frequently apply OMI (= complement), you cycle between element and complement, which keeps you in the coset structure of a particular subgroup.

5. **Next direction:** The OMI delta dominance is the sharpest single-metric signal found so far. The question is whether this is a consequence of f1 optimization (high f1 ↔ many OMI deltas) or an independent constraint. Test: among sequences with f1 ≥ 1.70, what percentile is KW's OMI delta count? If still extreme, it's a genuine additional constraint.
