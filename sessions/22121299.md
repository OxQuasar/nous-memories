The {4,2,2,2,2} principle: most of the time is background (Earth), rare periods carry signal.

```
Default state: reduced sizing, mean-reversion bias, tight stops
Detected regime shift: full sizing, directional bias, wider stops
```

The algo doesn't need to know *what* the regime is in five-phase terms. It needs a binary: background or signal? That's mechanizable — volatility breakout, correlation regime change, volume anomaly. The system runs conservatively most of the time and activates fully when conditions are genuinely unusual.

This captures the scarcity principle: directional periods are rare, so the system should be structurally skeptical and structurally responsive.

### 3. The exception operation as circuit breaker

The rarest judgment operation (5%) but the most important: override when something's wrong. This is the most directly implementable:

- Correlation breakdown (assets that should move together diverge)
- Liquidity withdrawal (spread widening, depth thinning)
- Volatility regime mismatch (signal from a calm regime applied during a volatile one)
- Calendar anomalies (FOMC, expiry, month-end)

These are *negative* conditions — not "take this trade" but "don't take any trade." The algo flags them and pauses. This is the 5% exception operation fully mechanized.

### 4. The gap as LLM layer

The operations that genuinely require judgment — analogy (40%) and weighting (17.5%) — can't be mechanized in the traditional sense. But an LLM can approximate them:

- **Analogy:** Feed the current market state (price action, recent history, technical levels, news context) and ask "what historical situations does this resemble? How did they resolve?" The LLM does contextual pattern matching — not mechanical indicator comparison, but compositional situational assessment.

- **Weighting (真/形色):** "Given these confirming and disconfirming factors, how confident should we be that this signal is genuine?" The LLM can assess the *quality* of the evidence, not just its quantity.

- **External context:** "What regime factors might affect this signal?" The LLM can incorporate narrative context (earnings season, geopolitical tension, positioning extremes) that indicators can't see.

The architecture would be:

```
Algo layer (11%):
  - Signal generation
  - Regime classification
  - Risk limits
  - Exception/circuit breakers
        ↓
LLM layer (89%):
  - Analogy: "what does this look like?"
  - Integration: "do the channels agree?"
  - Weighting: "真 or 形色?"
  - External: "what context matters?"
        ↓
Decision: trade / don't trade / modified sizing
```

The algo constrains. The LLM navigates. The gap between them is formalized, not eliminated.

### The honest caveat

The I Ching's 80/20 boundary works because the practitioner has years of training, deep familiarity with the texts, and situational awareness that no model fully captures. An LLM approximating the judgment layer is exactly that — an approximation. The research showed the residual is compositional and non-decomposable (R147, R149). An LLM might capture compositional meaning better than any feature-based system, but it's still an instrument measuring something that may require human cognition to fully navigate.

The strongest version might be: algo + LLM + human, where the algo generates candidates, the LLM filters and contextualizes, and the human makes the final 真/形色 call on the filtered set. Three layers, like the I Ching's three: algebra constrains, interface translates, practitioner navigates.
