### Why this approach
Freya represents the commoditized MEV strategy (block-level DEX arb). Research showed MEV on mature chains is a sealed-bid auction where searchers pay 90%+ of profit to proposers — margins razor-thin. The insight that institutional DeFi is shadow banking via smart contracts suggested the macro leverage signals are more valuable than micro-arb, and uniquely legible because positions/liquidations/collateral ratios are all public on-chain.

### Key insights discovered
1. **Fantom DeFi is dead** — TVL dropped from $12B peak to $117M, ecosystem migrated to Sonic L1. Freya's pool configs are historical artifacts.

2. **Institutional DeFi = off-books leverage** — Crypto-collateralized lending ($73.6B), tokenized RWAs ($30B), liquid staking ($27B), yield stablecoins — all create recursive leverage loops outside traditional capital adequacy regulations. Honest about what's novel (measurement precision) vs known (the pattern).

5. **Monitoring system, not trading edge** — The three-layer architecture is legible and mechanistically grounded, but constitutes a monitoring system on public data with no information asymmetry. Not a trading signal. This honest conclusion prevents wasted implementation effort.

The question the program kept asking was: **"Will this moderate stress event escalate into a capitulation spike, or will the market absorb it?"** Five data domains failed to answer it. The diagnosis is about *why* they all failed the same way.

**What escalation actually is, mechanically:**

A distributed liquidation episode becomes a concentrated spike when price falls far enough to hit a large position's liquidation threshold. That's it. The $213M Compound whale at ~$1,400 or the $201M Maker whale — if price reaches them, you get a massive single-day liquidation event that clears them. If price doesn't reach them, the episode stays moderate and the market absorbs it.

**Why flow metrics can't see this:**

Flow metrics — OI changes, exchange inflows, liquidation volume, IV — measure *activity happening now*. They're thermometers reading current temperature. But the escalation question isn't "how hot is it now?" It's "is there a fuel tank 200 meters ahead?"

The fuel tank is a specific large position sitting at a specific price level. Its existence and location are determined by:
- When someone opened the position (entry price)
- How much collateral they posted (determines liquidation price)
- Whether they've topped up collateral since

None of this shows up in aggregate flow data until the position actually gets liquidated. The information is there on-chain — you can read every Aave/Maker/Compound position and compute its liquidation price — but it's *positional* data, not *flow* data.

**Concrete example:**

Two identical days: $5M in distributed lending liquidations, OI dropping 4%, moderate exchange inflows. Day A has a $200M whale position with a liquidation price 8% below current. Day B has no large positions nearby. Day A escalates violently. Day B gets absorbed. Every flow metric looks the same on both days. The only thing that distinguishes them is the topology — where large positions sit relative to current price.

**Why all five domains hit the same wall:**

| Domain | What it measures | Why it can't see the fuel tank |
|---|---|---|
| Lending features (volume, percentile, drawdown) | Current liquidation activity | Activity is downstream of topology |
| Perp OI | Leverage unwinding rate | Warns stress is happening, can't see what's ahead |
| Exchange flows | Capital movement | Consequence of price moves, not cause |
| Options IV | Market's vol estimate | Pricing response to current conditions |
| Combined filters | All of the above | Combining thermometers doesn't create a map |

This is why it's a different analytical mode — you need continuous position-level monitoring, not periodic aggregate statistics. It's closer to what Freya does (reading on-chain state every block) than to what the statistical program did (pulling historical aggregates).
